Field Dependence of the Resonant Absorption Frequency¶

Using a distilled water sample, the audio oscillator was set to an 80 Hz triangle wave at maximum amplitude. The RF Frequency was set near 14.29 MHz.

The block diagram is shown below:

At room temperature, the number of protons in the lower energy level ($N^+$) is slightly greater than the higher energy level ($N^-$). The proportion is given by a Boltzmann distribution:

$$\frac{N^-}{N^+} = e^{\frac{-E}{kT}}$$

where $E= h \gamma B$ is the energy between states.

The oscilloscope displays two peaks at resonance frequency since we are performing a linear sweep forwards and backwards.

CW NMR Experiment:

  1. Constant field; varied frequency
  2. Constant frequency; magnetic field sweep

Gyromagnetic Ratio: 29.0251689331 MHz/kG

Note that there is a linear relationship between the magnetic field strength and resonance frequency (allowing for a tolerance of $\pm 0.1 kG$ and $\pm 200 Hz$ respectively). The inverse slope of this line yields the gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$ for $^1{H}$ since,

$$E = h \gamma B \text{ and } E = h \nu \implies$$$$ \nu = \gamma B \text{ where } \omega = 2 \pi \nu$$

and so $\gamma = 29.02 \cdot 10^7\, \text{Rad} \, s^{-1} T^{-1}$, which is comparable to the tabulated value $26.75 \cdot 10^7 \, \text{Rad} \, s^{-1} T^{-1}$.

The current in the Helmholtz coils (by using the 1 Ohm resistor in series with the coils) can be calculated in the following manner:

$$V = IR$$$$188 \text{ mV} = I(1 \Omega)$$

The formula for the magnetic field produced by the coils is:

$$ B = \bigg(\frac{4}{5}\bigg)^\frac{3}{2} \frac{\mu_0 n I}{R} $$

where $n$ is the number of turns of the coil, $R$ is the radius of the coils (equal to the separation distance between solenoids), and $\mu_0$ is the magnetic permeability. So,

$$ B = \bigg(\frac{4}{5}\bigg)^\frac{3}{2} \frac{ (4 \pi \cdot 10^{-7} H \cdot m^{-1})(200)(0.188 A) }{0.0175 m} = 0.001932 \,T = 19.32 \,G$$

This value represents roughly 8.8 divisions on the oscilloscope. Since the sample's linewidth (FWHM) spanned roughly 0.4 divisions, the linewidth is ~0.9 Gauss.

Lock-in Enhanced Study of the Absorption Profiles¶

The apparatus is shown below:

The audio oscillator was set to a 200 Hz sine wave with an amplitude of $2 V_{pp}$ so that the magnetic field sweep is less than the resonance linewidth.

The lock-in amplifier detects only signals in phase with the reference and improves the signal to noise ratio.

Lock-in Amplifier¶

  • Phase-sensitive detection
  • Low-pass filter

$$V_{psd} = V_{sig} \sin{(2\pi f_{s} + \theta_{sig})} V_{ref} \sin{(2\pi f_{r} + \theta_{ref})} = $$$$\frac{1}{2} V_{sig}V_L \sin{(2\pi[f_{s}-f_{r}] + \theta_{sig} - \theta_{ref})} - \frac{1}{2} V_{sig}V_L \sin{(2\pi[f_{s}+f_{r}] + \theta_{sig} + \theta_{ref})}$$

Linewidth vs Modulation Frequency¶

  • The modulation amplitude was set by the variable attenuator.
  • The output of the lock-in amplifier is the derivative of the absorption profile.
  • The linewidth can be measured by looking at the derivative curve's peaks.

The exponential will be fit to the first four peaks. To fit a curve of the form $y=Ae^{Bx}$, we take the logarithm of both sides so that $\log{y}=\log{A}+Bx$. Then, simply fit $\log{y}$ against $x$.
